
**Abstract**  
X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS) is a powerful tool in the study of the local atomic environment in condensed matter. It has been often applied to the study of semiconductor heterostructures and nanostructures, significantly contributing to their characterization at the local level and to the understanding of the relation between atomic structure and physical properties.

This chapter begins with an introduction to XAFS as a tool for the determination of local structure; the physical origin of the fine structure is illustrated and the present understanding of X-ray absorption spectra in the framework of multiple scattering theory is outlined. The second section contains a description of the diverse experimental setups and detection schemes, which can be used in the field of semiconductor science; an effort has been made to make this section both as complete and up-to-date as possible, so that it can serve as a useful reference, also outside the field of semiconductor physics. In the third section, a review of the use of XAFS to study semiconductor heterostructures and nanostructures is presented, covering bond length variations in strained heterostructures, local atomic environments in nanostructures (Ge islands, embedded nanoparticles, porous Si, and related systems), nitride heterostructures, and thin films, and finally dilute alloys heterostructures, that is dilute nitrides and dilute magnetic semiconductors.

**Keywords:** X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS), synchrotron radiation, nanostructures, heterostructures, local atomic structure

## 1. Introduction to X-ray absorption spectroscopy for local structural studies
In this section an introduction to X-ray absorption fine structure is reported. After a review of some basic phenomenology, the origin of the fine structure in X-ray absorption spectra is described. An outline of multiple scattering (MS) theory is presented and, in this framework, the extended and near-edge regions of the X-ray absorption spectrum are discussed.

### 1.1. Outline and origin of the fine structure in X-ray absorption spectra
In the X-ray and vacuum ultraviolet (VUV) spectral regions, photoelectric absorption is the dominant interaction mechanism between electromagnetic radiation and matter. An X-ray absorption spectrum is a measurement of the absorption coefficient of a sample, Œº(hŒΩ), or of a quantity to which it is directly proportional, as a function of the energy of the impinging photons hŒΩ. One can determine (conceptually and experimentally) the absorption coefficient by measuring the flux of monochromatic photons incident on and transmitted by a sample of thickness x, I‚ÇÄ and I_T, respectively; these quantities are related by Œº(hŒΩ)x = ‚àíln[I_T(hŒΩ)/I‚ÇÄ(hŒΩ)] via the relation I_T(hŒΩ) = I‚ÇÄ(hŒΩ) e^(‚àíŒº(hŒΩ)x) (1). The product Œº(hŒΩ)x (a quantity proportional to the absorbance commonly used in VUV and visible spectroscopy) can be obtained by inverting this expression. 

The overall absorption coefficient of a sample is related to the atomic concentrations c_i and absorption cross sections œÉ_i(hŒΩ) of the constituent atoms by Œº(hŒΩ) = Œ£_i c_i‚ÄâœÉ_i(hŒΩ) (2). 

X-ray absorption spectra exhibit sharp absorption edges which occur at the energies at which excitation of particular core levels becomes energetically allowed. The energies of absorption edges are characteristic of each element; for example, excitation of electrons from 1s, 2s, 2p‚ÇÅ/‚ÇÇ, and 2p‚ÇÉ/‚ÇÇ states correspond to the K, L_I, L_II, and L_III absorption edges, respectively.

The presence of fine structure in X-ray absorption spectra has been known since 1920; this fine structure shows up as a modulation of Œº(hŒΩ) at energies greater than the absorption edge, the relative magnitude of which can be as high as ~10% and decays with increasing energy from the edge itself. Until the 1970s the field was plagued by contradictory experimental data and uncertain physical interpretation. Two decisive factors subsequently contributed to asserting XAFS as a valuable structural tool: the availability of brilliant and broadband synchrotron radiation sources and the development of an approximate but simple and useful point-scattering theory. The ‚Äúmodern‚Äù era of XAFS is usually traced to the paper by Sayers et al. Some historical reviews have been published recently and the interested reader is referred to them for details on the evolution of the technique; the introductory paragraph of the paper by Filipponi contains a compact historical summary.

The physical origin of XAFS in the framework of scattering theory can be understood quite easily. Consider an atom bound in a molecule or solid. In the one-electron approximation, the core level photoelectric absorption process is described in terms of the initial state consisting of the impinging photon of energy hŒΩ plus the electron in the core atomic orbital characterized by a wavefunction œà_i with a binding energy E_B and a final state consisting of a core hole plus an excited electron characterized by a wavefunction œà_f with an energy E_f. Depending on the photon energy, the electron can be excited to a discrete and bound final state or to a continuum state, and the interaction of this outgoing electron wave with the surrounding atoms gives rise to the oscillatory fine structure observed in XAFS.# Continuum and Unbound Final State

#### 1.4 XANES or near-edge X-ray absorption fine structure
In the near-edge spectral region, the MS series does not converge and thus it is not possible to express the cross section as a sum of sine functions. A simulation of the XANES region requires the inversion of the matrix (I ‚Äì T‚ÄâG) and is relatively time-consuming in terms of computer calculations. The XANES line shape is sensitive both to the details of the scattering potential (because of the low energy of the photoelectron) and to the three-dimensional positions of atoms contained within a radius greater than that necessary to reproduce EXAFS. As a consequence, quantitative analysis of XANES is not generally possible at present.

An interpretation of XANES spectra in terms of electronic structure is also possible. If one neglects the energy dependence of the matrix element in a limited range of the near-edge region, the absorption cross section is proportional to the density of final states (in the presence of the core hole), selected in angular momentum by the transition rules and projected on the absorbing atomic species. This interpretation links XANES to electronic‚Äêstructure calculations and is often used for small molecules.

XANES finds widespread application in many fields, often with a ‚Äúfinger‚Äêprinting‚Äù approach in which the spectrum of a sample is compared to those of reference compounds of known structure. For example, XANES spectra of transition‚Äêmetal compounds exhibit clear differences with oxidation state and coordination. Analysis of the XANES spectrum provides information on the site symmetry of the absorbing atom (e.g., tetrahedral or octahedral) and on its oxidation state.

Because XANES is sensitive to three-dimensional atomic geometry (while the dominant contributions to EXAFS are essentially one-dimensional) and because XANES spectra are more easily recorded than EXAFS ones (especially for dilute elements), quantitative interpretation of XANES would be of great interest. In semiconductors, recent work has shown that comparison between experimental spectra and ab initio simulations can determine the three-dimensional structure of defects, such as the nitrogen‚Äìhydrogen complex in dilute nitride alloys. Significant progress has also been made in structural fitting of XANES spectra for biomolecules.Let us conclude mentioning that in the important case of the L-edges of transition metals, in which a p electron makes a transition to a narrow d band, multiplet theory is used to interpret the spectra because of strong electron-correlation effects. A useful review on these aspects has been written by de Groot [32]. L-edge spectra have been used to study the local structure of Mn in dilute magnetic semiconductors (DMSs), as will be described in Section 3.

## 2. Detection schemes and experimental setups relevant for semiconductor research
In this section, the main experimental setups relevant for semiconductor research are described. An essential requirement for any XAFS experiment is a brilliant synchrotron-radiation source and appropriate optics to provide a focal spot on the sample with the required photon flux, bandpass, stability, divergence, and spot size. Considerable progress in this field has been made in recent years [33‚Äì38]. Here, we focus on the experimental chamber itself and the appropriate detection schemes. The objective of any XAFS measurement is to record, as a function of photon energy, a quantity directly proportional to the cross section of the atom of interest in its specific environment or phase. Several methods have been devised and are described below. For diffraction anomalous fine structure (DAFS) we refer to Chapter 11 by Proietti et al.

### 2.1 Transmission geometry
A transmission measurement of XAFS is the most commonly used. It relies on measuring the flux of monochromatic photons incident, \(I_0\), and transmitted, \(I_T\), by a sample of thickness \(x\). The inversion of Eq. (1) gives  
\( \mu(E)\,x = \ln\frac{I_0(E)}{I_T(E)}\) (18)  
where \(\mu(E)\) is the absorption coefficient. The main requirement for this measurement is that the discontinuity in \(\mu x\) be in the range 1‚Äì2 and that the lateral homogeneity of the sample be high enough to avoid ‚Äúthickness effects.‚Äù In practice, sample thicknesses must be 1‚Äì10 Œºm with homogeneities within a fraction of this; hence transmission measurements are usually not practical in the soft-X-ray range, where required thicknesses are too small to fabricate homogeneous samples. The transmission technique can be applied when the atom of interest is not dilute or is not at or near the surface; its main advantage is very good signal-to-noise ratios, as it relies on high photon fluxes (10^9‚Äì10^11 photons/s). The most commonly used detectors for transmission measurements are gas ionization chambers.

In semiconductor heterostructures and nanostructures, one often studies ultrathin films, nanolayers, or low-dimensional structures on or near the surface. Pristine samples are thus not usually appropriate for transmission mode, as the substrate or matrix background would be too high. As a notable exception, Ridgway‚Äôs group [40] devised specific sample-preparation procedures involving etching of the substrate and stacking of various layers of the sample of interest to achieve an equivalent thickness suitable for transmission measurements; by combining this with low temperatures (which reduce the Debye‚ÄìWaller factor, Eq. (14)), these authors obtained data of excellent signal-to-noise ratio.

### 2.2 Decay channels: fluorescence and electron yield
Reduction of background absorption from the matrix can be achieved by selectively recording an atom-specific signal. One can exploit the decay of the photoexcited atom, which occurs either via emission of a fluorescence photon (characteristic radiation) or of an Auger electron; both have energies that depend on the atomic number of the photoexcited atom.

#### 2.2.1 Fluorescence yield detection
In the fluorescence-yield (FY) detection mode [41], a photon detector records the fluorescence intensity as a function of energy. In the most common geometry with horizontally polarized X-rays, the detector is placed in the horizontal plane at right angles to the incident beam to minimize elastic scattering. In this geometry, the fluorescence intensity \(I_f(E)\) of atom A embedded in matrix M for a sample thickness \(d\) is  
\( I_f(E) = I_0(E)\,\frac{\varepsilon_f\,\Delta\Omega\,\mu_A(E)}{\mu_{\rm tot}(E)+\mu_{\rm tot}(E_f)\tan\theta}\Bigl[1 - \exp\bigl(-[\mu_{\rm tot}(E)+\mu_{\rm tot}(E_f)\tan\theta]\,d\bigr)\Bigr]\) (19)  
where \(\varepsilon_f\) is the FY, \(\Delta\Omega\) the detector‚Äôs solid angle, \(E_f\) the fluorescence-photon energy, \(\theta\) the angle between the beam and sample surface, and \(\mu_{\rm tot}=\mu_A+\mu_M\) the total absorption coefficient. Because \(\mu_{\rm tot}\) depends on \(\mu_A\), Eq. (19) is not generally proportional to \(\mu_A(E)\). Proportionality is recovered in two limits: thin samples (series expansion of the exponential truncated at linear term, typically \(d<100\) nm) and thick samples in which atom A is dilute (\(\mu_{\rm tot}\approx\mu_M\), often below a few atomic %). If these conditions are not met, fluorescence data can be corrected for nonproportionality (‚Äúself-absorption‚Äù) as described by various authors [42‚Äì47].

Many detector types have been used for FY mode, but the most common are solid-state Si:Li or (nowadays) hyperpure Ge diodes.## 2.2.1 Fluorescence Yield Detection

Multielement arrays have an energy resolution in the range of 150‚Äì200 eV at 6 keV, a reasonable trade-off between counting rate and background rejection; for example, the spectrum of photons emitted from a sample consisting of 10^16 Sn atoms/cm¬≤ implanted in SiO‚ÇÇ recorded on the GILDA beamline of ESRF shows elastic scattering, inelastic (Compton) scattering and Sn fluorescence clearly identified. As pulse-counting electronics is used, care must be taken to avoid or correct dead-time effects.  

Because both the probe and the detected signals are penetrating X-rays, fluorescence yield (FY) is the best choice to study dilute elements in the bulk of materials. This has led to a wide application of XAFS to the study of dopants in semiconductors. The lowest concentration measurable in a FY-XAFS experiment ultimately depends on the magnitude of the inelastic scattering background in the specific experimental geometry. For the specific case of the LIII-edge of Er in GaAs or InP, the lowest dose measurable is 1‚Äì5 √ó 10^14 atoms/cm¬≤. In the more general case, the lowest detectable dose or thickness depends on the absorbing atom, the matrix and the experimental geometry; XAFS spectra on thicknesses equivalent to 1‚Äì0.1 monolayers are nowadays measurable with adequate signal-to-noise ratio, and even lower concentrations can be measured in specific cases.

## 2.2.2 Electron Yield Detection
The decay of the core hole can also occur with the emission of an Auger electron. This Auger electron creates a cascade of lower-energy (‚Äúsecondary‚Äù) electrons due to multiple ionization and inelastic scattering processes. Various electron-yield detection methods exist, depending on which electrons are detected and how:
- **Total electron yield (TEY)**  
  The simplest method, often used in the soft X-ray region, consists of measuring the drain current using an electrometer that connects the sample to ground.  
- **Partial electron yield (PEY)**  
  Only electrons within a given energy range are detected by means of retarding grids and electron detectors.  
- **Conversion electron yield (CEY)**  
  In the hard X-ray range, where cross sections are smaller, the signal due to radiationless decay is recorded using a He-filled ionization chamber in which the sample acts as the anode; He gas acts as an electron multiplier and an amplified signal is recorded from the cathode.  
- **Auger electron yield (AEY)**  
  The number of Auger electrons themselves is recorded directly; this requires an electron analyzer operating in ultrahigh-vacuum conditions and is used only in surface XAFS experiments.

All electron detection modes are surface sensitive, due to the limited electron mean free path, making them particularly useful for investigating near-surface regions or interfaces. Depth sensitivity in TEY is typically a few hundreds to a few thousands of √•ngstr√∂ms. Comparisons between TEY and CEY show that TEY is more surface-sensitive than CEY.  

Spectra recorded using electron detection methods may suffer from ‚Äúself-absorption effects,‚Äù similar to those in FY, though generally less severe because the electron absorption coefficient is much higher than the photon absorption coefficient and thus dominates the denominator in Eq. (19). Correction procedures have been described in the literature.

## 2.3 Alternative Detection Methods
A number of nonconventional methods to detect XAFS have been proposed, each with its own advantages, limitations and pitfalls. Those relevant to the investigation of semiconductors are briefly reviewed here; results on specific systems are discussed in Section 3.

### 2.3.1 Optical Detection
When excited by an X-ray beam, many samples emit visible or near-visible radiation. X-ray excited optical luminescence (XEOL) and the measurement of the XAFS spectrum via the resulting photoluminescence yield (PLY) have attracted considerable attention, owing to the possibility of determining the local structure of only the atoms involved in the luminescence emission, thus adding site selectivity to XAFS.  

XEOL was first observed in CaF‚ÇÇ; the PLY-XAFS spectrum actually exhibited an inverted jump (i.e., a decrease rather than an increase of the PLY at the edge). The roles of the attenuation lengths for the X-ray and optical photons and the sample composition in determining this behavior were subsequently understood.  

A fundamental issue is the effective site selectivity of PLY-XAFS: one must verify that the change of the PLY at the absorption edge of a particular atom is solely due to absorbers in the optically active centers. In many cases‚Äîparticularly in scintillators or ionic crystals‚Äîthe optically active center may be different or distant from the selected absorbing atoms. In a mixture of ZnS‚ÄìZnSe powders, where the two components emit at different wavelengths, site selectivity was clearly demonstrated; conversely, the important role of interion excitation has been shown in other doped samples.### 2.3.2 Electrical detection

The electrical detection of XAFS has been investigated by several groups with the objective of determining whether the local structure of only those atoms contributing to the electrical signal due to defects could be probed, leading to a potentially site‚Äêselective version of XAFS.

Photocurrent‚Äêdetected XAFS spectra have been reported by Boehme et al. and Hu et al. for an Al‚ÄìGaAs diode and for bulk GaAs, respectively. Their common conclusion is that, for these concentrated samples, once corrections for sample thickness are applied, the spectra contain the same structural information as traditional transmission‚Äêmode measurements.

Ishii et al. proposed a capacitance‚Äêdetected version of XAFS using a Se‚Äêdoped AlGaAs‚ÄìAl diode exhibiting the so‚Äêcalled DX deep‚Äêlevel electron trap. Since changes in capacitance arise from electrons localized in the traps, they argued it should be possible to determine the local structure of only those atoms near the trap. Indeed, their Ga‚Äêedge spectra recorded in fluorescence and capacitance modes exhibit significant differences. Variations of the capacitance method have also been proposed: by changing the diode bias potential one can achieve depth selectivity, while scanning capacitance probes could provide lateral resolution on the length scale of an AFM.

The site selectivity of capacitance‚Äêdetected XAFS was challenged in detail by Bollmann et al. They studied samples with both DX deep‚Äêlevel and antisite defects and found no significant differences between fluorescence‚Äê and capacitance‚Äêmode spectra. Modeling of the X‚Äêray‚Äêinduced current and capacitance changes led them to conclude that defect centers can be ionized by electrons generated by core ionization of atoms distant from the defect, so no true site selectivity is achieved. More work is needed to resolve this issue before the detection mode can be widely accepted.

### 2.3.3 Beta environmental fine structure
In the context of precise neutrino‚Äêmass measurements, it has been suggested that the Œ≤‚Äêdecay spectrum of a nucleus embedded in a crystal or molecule could exhibit fine structure analogous to XAFS: the emitted Œ≤ particle (electron) is scattered by neighboring atoms, and interference effects modulate the decay rate, leading to so‚Äêcalled beta environmental fine structure (BEFS). BEFS was detected in the decay spectrum of ¬π‚Å∏‚Å∑Re, and since Œ≤‚Äêemitting nuclei include tritium, ¬π‚Å¥C and several metals, BEFS could be highly valuable for materials physics and chemistry. The Re BEFS spectrum was later analyzed quantitatively, showing good agreement with the known crystal structure; an open issue concerning the angular momentum of the final state (s or p) has been addressed more recently. BEFS is potentially very powerful‚Äîespecially for studying the local environment of hydrogen in semiconductors (where traditional XAFS is impractical because of the low K‚Äêedge binding energy)‚Äîbut it has yet to become a routine tool.

## 2.4.2 Surface X-ray absorption fine structure
The potential of XAFS to determine the local structure of interfaces deposited in situ in an ultrahigh‚Äêvacuum environment was realized early on [59,60]. As far as use of the EXAFS spectral region is concerned, this has led to the acronym SEXAFS for ‚Äúsurface EXAFS.‚Äù The specific advantage of surface XAFS for the study of heterostructures is the possibility of determining the atomic environment in the first few coordination shells of an excited atom in the epilayer (with an error of the order of 0.01 √Ö for the interatomic distances), during the formation of the interface, without exposure to the atmosphere. This information is crucial for a correct physical modeling of interface properties and now can be usefully compared to ab initio structural simulations. By exploiting the dependence of the XAFS cross section on the relative orientation of the sample and the linearly polarized synchrotron radiation beam, the structure parallel and perpendicular to the growth plane can be determined [94,95]. When information on the long‚Äêrange order is necessary, surface XAFS can be usefully coupled to grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (XRD). Surface XAFS has been reviewed a number of times; some of these reviews are listed in Refs [96‚Äì98].

Surface XAFS relies on detection schemes which are able to distinguish the signal originating in the interface from that originating from the bulk: TEY, PEY or FY, often combined with grazing incidence, all of which have been described previously. Of course, the further requirement is of an ultrahigh‚Äêvacuum chamber connected to the beamline and equipped with all the instrumentation required to prepare an atomically clean and ordered surface and to deposit and characterize ultrathin epilayers. A particularly detailed description of a well‚Äêequipped surface XAFS apparatus has been reported by Oyanagi et al. [99].

## 2.4.3 Micro-XAFS
As brighter synchrotron sources have become available, it has become possible to obtain smaller focal spot sizes and to design instruments capable of increasingly higher spatial resolution. In fact, the field of X‚Äêray microscopy has progressed very rapidly in recent years; lateral resolutions of the order of 10 nm have been obtained with photoelectron imaging techniques, e.g., the photo emission electron microscope or PEEM (see Locatelli et al. [100] for an example of a spectromicroscopy beamline using photoelectron imaging, and Ratto et al. [100] for an application to Ge dots on Si).

There is a great interest in performing XAFS with high lateral resolution (micro‚ÄêXAFS) as it allows a new level of description of heterogeneous samples, combining microscopy with the atomic‚Äêscale structural information obtainable from XAFS; with sufficiently small focal spots the local atomic environment of single nanostructures might be determined. The challenge is to record XAFS spectra free of systematic errors and with sufficiently good signal‚Äêto‚Äênoise ratio. At present, XAFS spectra can be recorded with spot sizes of the order of a few square micrometers, for example on the ID22 beamline [101] of the ESRF or on the LUCIA beamline [102] of the SLS; micro‚ÄêXAFS is often complemented with microfluorescence mapping. Applications of micro‚ÄêXAFS to semiconductors have been relatively limited so far, but significant progress is expected. Micro‚ÄêXAFS is particularly useful when samples exhibit lateral inhomogeneities; many applications in semiconductors have been on samples whose growth has yet to be optimized, for example dilute magnetic semiconductors based on GaN (see Section 3). In several synchrotron radiation laboratories, efforts are under way to combine information derived from XAFS with the nanometer‚Äêlevel lateral resolution obtainable by using atomic force microscope tips [103]. While photon‚Äêbased techniques may never reach the sub‚Äê√•ngstr√∂m spatial resolution of electron microscopy, recent advances represent many orders of magnitude improvement in spatial resolution. In the future, micro‚ÄêXAFS will certainly become a powerful tool in materials science, complementing spatially averaged techniques which have been key to the success and widespread use of X‚Äêrays for structural determination.

# 3 A review of the use of XAFS in the field of semiconductor heterostructures and nanostructures
In the field of materials science, new growth and deposition schemes are continuously devised with the objective of obtaining samples and devices with novel physical properties. The role of highly sensitive and sophisticated methods such as XAFS is to provide a description of the local atomic environment which can form the structural basis for understanding the physical properties. In the past 20 years, XAFS has developed from an intriguing physical phenomenon to a tool that is used in many branches of science. In this section, an attempt is made to review the use of XAFS in the study of the local atomic environment of semiconductor heterostructures and nanostructures. This is a rather wide field and the choice of examples is partially a personal one. Not all aspects of semiconductor science are covered; for example, the important application of XAFS to the study of dopants, implanted atoms and defects is not included.### 3.1 Bond lengths in strained III‚ÄìV heterostructures

The study of the effect of strain on the local atomic structure, especially on bond lengths, is an area in which XAFS has provided key results, clarifying what initially seemed a rather confusing situation. The applicability of XAFS to study small bond‚Äêlength variations in (unstrained) semiconductor pseudobinary alloys was demonstrated in pioneering papers in the 1980s [104,105]. The main result of these investigations is that bond lengths exhibit a variation with relative concentration which is significantly weaker than that exhibited by the lattice parameter. In fact, the lattice parameter of a pseudobinary semiconductor alloy of composition A‚ÇìB‚ÇÅ‚Çã‚ÇìC and zinc blende structure generally obeys Vegard‚Äôs law,  
a(x) = x a_AC + (1 ‚Äì x) a_BC‚ÄÉ(25)  
where a_AC and a_BC are the lattice parameters of the binary compounds. Experimentally, the bond lengths R_AC and R_BC exhibit a much weaker dependence on x. This can be qualitatively understood as due to the ‚Äúrigidity‚Äù of semiconductor bonds and implies the existence of local distortions (variations of bond angles).  

Cai and Thorpe [106] proposed a comprehensive theory of bond‚Äêlength variation in semiconductor alloys, able to reproduce existing experimental results and with predictive ability. A basic ingredient of their work is the assumption that there are no differences between bond‚Äêstretching and bond‚Äêbending force constants in elemental tetrahedra centered on different atoms, and that an average value applies in all cases. XAFS is the appropriate tool to study this problem because of its chemical selectivity and high resolution at the short‚Äêdistance scale.  

A pair distribution function (PDF) analysis of XRD patterns has also recently been shown to determine interatomic distances in bulk InGa‚ÇÅ‚Çã‚ÇìAs‚Çì samples [107], and ab initio structure determination of randomly oriented nanostructures is in principle feasible [108]. The PDF can be obtained for bulk ZnSe‚ÇÅ‚Çã‚ÇìTe‚Çì from neutron diffraction data [109]. An advantage of PDF analysis is its combined short‚Äê and medium‚Äêscale sensitivity, while a limitation is its applicability to thin crystalline epilayers.  

When a cubic epilayer is grown pseudomorphically on a substrate with a different lattice parameter, below a certain critical thickness its unit cell undergoes a tetragonal distortion. The perpendicular and parallel strains are defined as  
Œµ‚ä• = (a‚ä• ‚Äì a_f)/a_f  
Œµ‚à• = (a‚à• ‚Äì a_f)/a_f  
where a_f is the epilayer‚Äôs free (unstrained) lattice parameter. For two‚Äêdimensional growth on a (001) substrate the strains are related by  
Œµ‚ä• = ‚Äì2 (C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ/C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÅ) Œµ‚à•‚ÄÉ(26)  
with C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ and C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÅ the elastic constants. The open issue in the early 1990s was the effect of this long‚Äêrange strain at the local scale‚Äîon individual bond lengths. Given the known rigidity of semiconductor bonds, does strain affect bond lengths? In alloy epilayers, is the effect the same for different bonds, and how does it combine with alloy‚Äêinduced bond‚Äêlength variations? XAFS, often in fluorescence yield (FY) mode and sometimes in grazing incidence to enhance surface sensitivity and reject substrate contributions [110], proved to be the technique of choice.  

Bond‚Äêlength strain has been studied in SiGe/Si(001) alloys and, most clearly, in III‚ÄìV semiconductor heterostructures. One approach measures bond lengths in a binary or fixed‚Äêcomposition alloy epilayer as a function of thickness, to observe strain relaxation above the critical thickness. Alternatively, for an alloy epilayer below the critical thickness, bond lengths are measured as a function of composition: as the free lattice parameter varies according to Vegard‚Äôs law (25), one must distinguish strain‚Äêinduced bond‚Äêlength changes from those due to alloying.  

Initially, many authors [111‚Äì113] reported negligible bond‚Äêlength variations in strained layers, but with large error bars (¬±0.01‚Äì0.02 √Ö). The first clear report of strain‚Äêinduced bond‚Äêlength variation was by Kuwahara et al. [114], who measured the In‚ÄìAs bond length in InAs and InAs‚ÇÄ.‚ÇÜP‚ÇÄ.‚ÇÑ layers on InP(001). They found a clear compression at low thicknesses and an increase around the critical thickness for strain relaxation (see Fig. 7). Woicik et al. [115] obtained similar results for InAs/GaAs(001).  

Subsequent experiments on strained alloy layers as a function of composition provided definitive insight and led to a simple model for the effect. Romanato et al. [116] studied tensile and compressively strained In‚ÇìGa‚ÇÅ‚Çã‚ÇìAs epilayers on InP(001) (0.25 < x < 0.75), while Woicik et al. [117] investigated a buried In‚ÇÄ.‚ÇÇ‚ÇÇGa‚ÇÄ.‚Çá‚ÇàAs layer in GaAs, finding similar results. Romanato et al.‚Äôs data show that for strained layers the slope of the bond‚Äêlength vs. concentration plot is inverted compared to unstrained alloys (which follow Mikkelsen and Boyce‚Äôs classical results [104]). A simple model reproduces this remarkable effect.The strain-induced distortion for bonds in the [111] direction is predicted to be  
rst = a(x) / (2‚àö3) (1 ‚Äì C12/C11) Œµ‚Äñ (27)

The effects of alloying and strain are summed linearly. As can be seen, the agreement with the data is excellent. A consequence of this model is that different bonds (e.g., In‚ÄìAs and Ga‚ÄìAs) actually respond to strain in the same way, despite the fact that bond stretching force constants are significantly different; this is not surprising as the alloying-induced variations were reproduced with average values of the force constants [106]. More refined methods to calculate bond lengths in strained layers, based on a valence force field potential, have been published by Woicik [118] and d‚ÄôAcapito [119], with a similar degree of good success. The issue of the effect of strain on bond lengths can be considered as solved and measurements of variations of bond lengths in strained systems can now be used to determine strain, as will be described in the next section.

### 3.2 Atomic environment in nanostructures
XAFS has been used to determine the atomic environment in many semiconductor nanostructures. The applicability of XAFS to study the changes of the local environment in nanostructures as a function of their dimensions was recognized early in the development of the technique, as shown by the studies on metallic clusters performed in the 1980s [120‚Äì122]. These studies demonstrated that nanoparticles exhibit a contraction of bond lengths, a reduced average coordination number and an enhanced structural and vibrational disorder. XAFS is the technique of choice because its local sensitivity (which derives from the limited photoelectron mean free path and core hole lifetime) makes it extremely powerful in detecting even subtle changes in the local atomic environment; moreover, the method of analysis does not change with the degree of long-range order even in going from a dimer molecule to a perfect crystal, a situation which must be contrasted with the case of XRD, and there are no consequences of even great changes in the morphology of a nanoparticle on the analysis formalism. Here, the most important topics addressed using XAFS are reviewed.

#### 3.2.1 Ge quantum dots and islands on Si surfaces and related systems
The possibility of altering the physical properties of semiconductors by exploiting quantum size effects has led to the recent big interest in nanostructures. In this field, XAFS has provided key results, especially regarding the intermixing in Ge quantum dots and islands on Si. XAFS has the advantage of providing a local view of the atomic environment of the excited atom, thus providing quantitative information on intermixing and bond length strain in a direct fashion. The chemical sensitivity necessary to obtain information on intermixing can be obtained by XRD techniques only by exploiting anomalous dispersion. In this context, anomalous XRD at high momentum transfer has been successfully applied to determine composition and strain profiles of Ge islands on Si [123]; disentangling the contributions of strain, intermixing, and morphology to the XRD pattern required a significant amount of modeling. A limitation of XAFS for the ex situ study of uncapped dots and islands is that for thin samples the contribution from the surface oxide (or from other disordered phases) can be significant, which might complicate analysis; a solution is the use of a capping layer or performing an in situ experiment. In view of the advantages of XAFS in this field, a great deal of papers have been published, of which we review the most significant.

Ge islands on Si(001) are among the most studied systems. It is known that, due to the 4.2% lattice misfit, heteroepitaxial growth proceeds with the formation of a two-dimensional wetting layer, followed by the formation of dots which relieve the lattice strain (at the cost of an increase in surface energy); this is the so-called Stranski‚ÄìKrastanov growth mode. XAFS experiments [124] on Ge dots formed on Si(001) and Si(111) were among the first to detect the presence of atomic intermixing by any technique, a phenomenon which had been neglected up to that time; other evidence for intermixing from XAFS was provided by Kolobov et al. [125] and Erenburg et al. [126]. The experimental evidence is rather clear, asFigure 9 compares the Fourier transform (FT) of Ge K-edge spectra for bulk Ge, a Ge impurity in crystalline Si, and a representative sample of Ge dots on Si(001). The asymmetrical lineshape of the first peak (first coordination shell) indicates a significant number of Si neighbors around the average Ge atom; this has been confirmed by spectral fitting. It is now accepted that diffusion of Si atoms from the substrate reduces the strain energy associated with heteroepitaxial growth and is a key feature of quantum-dot formation. Hence, the appropriate growth mode for these dots is a modified Stranski‚ÄìKrastanow one. Motta et al. [128] monitored the evolution of intermixing on the Si(111) surface, finding enhanced interdiffusion with increasing substrate temperature. Direct information on intermixing from XAFS was also illustrated by Kovats et al. [129], who found that intermixing was inhibited when dangling bonds on the Si(111) surface were saturated by B atoms.

Kolobov et al. [130] compared XAFS to Raman spectroscopy for local studies of Ge quantum dots on Si, showing that the overlap of a Si-substrate Raman peak with the Ge signal limits Raman‚Äôs applicability. The same group detected considerable atomic intermixing in capped Ge dots; the extent depends on the capping-layer growth temperature [131] and can be eliminated by growth on SiO‚ÇÇ [132]. The second- and third-shell interatomic distances (obtained by including significant multiple‚Äêscattering paths) reflect the strained state of the dots. d‚ÄôAcapito et al. [133] also used bond lengths to quantify Ge-dot strain.

Atomic intermixing is not limited to group-IV dots: in III‚ÄìV systems, Galluppi et al. [134] first detected In/Ga intermixing in nominally pure InAs dots on GaAs by In K-edge XAS, correlating these structural measurements with photoluminescence. d‚ÄôAcapito et al. [135], using grazing‚Äêincidence measurements on 1.3‚Äì3 monolayer (ML) thick samples, confirmed these results and showed that bond lengths reliably indicate dot strain. Finally, Renevier et al. [136] contrasted InAs/GaAs dots (showing interdiffusion) with InAs/InP quantum wires, where no group-V interdiffusion was found.

### 3.2.2 Embedded semiconductor nanoparticles
Semiconductor nanoparticles embedded in a dielectric medium exhibit unique optical properties due to quantum‚Äêsize effects and increased surface-to-volume ratios. Ion implantation followed by thermal annealing is a flexible method to create such nanoparticles near the surface of a dielectric. Here we review XAFS studies of Ge, GaN and II‚ÄìVI nanoparticles in various (mostly amorphous) matrices.

Ridgway‚Äôs group studied Ge nanocrystals in silica and found two main results [137, 138]:
- For mean sizes of 14 nm, Ge retains tetrahedral coordination but shows enhanced Gaussian and non-Gaussian disorder compared to bulk crystalline Ge, highlighting XAFS sensitivity to short-range radial distribution.
- Ge nanocrystals amorphize under Si-ion irradiation at doses 100√ó smaller than required for bulk Ge. Fourier transforms of Ge K-edge XAFS data (Fig. 10) reveal strong damping of second- and third-shell signals (3‚Äì5 √Ö) in nanocrystals. Possible causes include interface-preferential amorphization, intrinsic nanocrystal instability, enhanced vacancy concentration, and ion-beam mixing.

Borsella et al. [139] fabricated GaN quantum dots (~5 nm) in crystalline (quartz, sapphire) or amorphous (silica) dielectrics by sequential Ga and N ion implantation (doses ~10^17 cm‚Åª¬≤) followed by NH‚ÇÉ or NH‚ÇÉ/H‚ÇÇ annealing (900‚Äì1200 ¬∞C, <1 h). The wurtzite‚Äêphase dots show quantum confinement in optical spectra. Ga K-edge EXAFS (Fig. 11) confirms local wurtzite structure but reveals reduced second-peak amplitude relative to the first, indicating partial Ga incorporation in clusters and some Ga‚ÄìO bonding in the matrix.

Hayes‚Äôs group studied CdS nanoparticles (2‚Äì6 nm) in borosilicate glass, formed by 0.1 wt% CdS doping, quenching and annealing (625‚Äì900 ¬∞C). These particles exhibit blue-shifted optical absorption edges. Cd-edge XAFS [140, 141] shows Cd both in nanoparticles and dispersed in the glass, with high structural disorder. Fourier transforms of samples (as-received and annealed 14 h at 700 ¬∞C, Fig. 12) display a shift to higher distances of the first peak upon annealing and absence of features beyond the first peak in the as-received sample. The first and second shell distances are within 0.2% of bulk values, indicating low overall strain.Interfacial tension, suggesting that the glass matrix is able to accommodate and relax strains, increasing the particles‚Äô stability.

### 3.2.3 Metallic nanostructures embedded in silica
Metallic nanocrystals embedded in amorphous SiO‚ÇÇ on Si(001) have been recently investigated in view of their nonlinear optical properties, with applications ranging from optical fibers, memories, and switching devices. XAFS has been employed by several groups to study the variations of the local structure as a function of particle size and processing, and its relation to the physical properties.

Spiga et al. [142] have investigated the formation and the structural properties of Sn nanocrystals produced by ion implantation in ultrathin silica films, using grazing‚Äêincidence XAFS in conjunction with M√∂ssbauer spectroscopy and transmission electron microscopy. The analysis of this system is complicated by the multiple oxidation states of Sn.### 3.2.3 Sn and Au Nanoclusters Formed by Ion Implantation and Annealing

Sn ions were implanted at 80 keV with a fluence of 10^16 cm^‚Äì2, positioning the peak of the implantation profile in the middle of an 85 nm‚Äìthick thermally grown SiO‚ÇÇ film. After implantation, samples were annealed at 800‚Äì1100 ¬∞C; resulting Sn particle sizes ranged from 7 to 17 nm. In the as-implanted state all Sn ions are oxidized, with Sn^2+ oxidation states predominant, while annealing induces the formation of Œ≤-Sn nanoclusters. XANES provides qualitative information on the oxidation state and EXAFS measures local structural parameters; grazing‚Äêincidence geometry enhances the signal from ultrathin films.

For particle sizes below 10 nm, a reduced coordination number and contraction of interatomic distances were observed. Even after annealing, complete precipitation of Sn into the metallic phase was not achieved, leaving a fraction of ions oxidized.

Kluth et al. studied Au nanocrystals fabricated by implanting Au ions at 4.5 MeV into 2 ¬µm‚Äìthick SiO‚ÇÇ at fluences from 10^16 to 3√ó10^17 cm^‚Äì2, followed by forming-gas annealing for 1 h at 1100 ¬∞C. Average Au particle sizes ranged from 2.5 to 10 nm. High-quality XAFS data revealed a bond‚Äêlength contraction of ~0.02 √Ö in smaller particles (with measurement uncertainty ‚Äì0.002 to ‚Äì0.005 √Ö), consistent with reports on nonembedded clusters and indicating negligible influence from the SiO‚ÇÇ matrix.

The same group later investigated the effect of Sb irradiation (2.3 MeV, doses 10^14‚Äì10^16 cm^‚Äì2) as a method to tune nanoparticle properties. At low doses, significant disorder and bond‚Äêlength expansion occur; at higher doses, Au atoms partly dissolve into the matrix and form very small clusters (dimers, trimers).

### 3.2.4 Porous Si and Related Systems
Porous Si (p-Si), typically obtained by anodic etching of Si in HF, exhibits visible photoluminescence. Its origin has been attributed either to quantum‚Äêsize effects or to oxide‚Äêrelated surface structures. XAFS‚Äîespecially in photoluminescence yield (PLY/XEOL) mode‚Äîhas been crucial in correlating atomic structure and luminescence.

- Sham‚Äôs group showed that TEY‚Äêdetected XAFS spectra are more sensitive to oxide, while PLY‚Äêdetected spectra resemble bulk Si, indicating that optically active p-Si locally retains the diamond structure.
- Pettifer et al. used time‚Äêresolved PLY‚ÄìXANES to distinguish environments: the fast blue luminescence component is linked to SiO‚ÇÇ‚Äêrich regions.
- The Trento group performed transmission‚Äêmode EXAFS on freshly etched p-Si (oxide removed by HF), confirming the diamond structure and finding increased Debye‚ÄìWaller factors with porosity. Comparison of TEY and PLY spectra showed that PLY‚Äêdetected EXAFS exhibits growing disorder with higher porosity, evidenced by decreasing amplitudes of the first three FT peaks.
- A partial PLY detection mode was proposed: by selecting emission energy (higher emission energy ‚Üî smaller emitting clusters), Dalba et al. demonstrated that the Si K‚Äêedge shifts linearly with emission energy. Since the edge corresponds to transitions from a core level to the conduction‚Äêband minimum, this supports the quantum‚Äêconfinement model for p-Si luminescence.from a few to a few tens of nanometers and lengths up to a few hundred microns. The wires are generally covered by an oxide layer. XAFS has provided an atomistic description of the structure of these systems. In particular, Zhang et al. [151] have studied Si NWs obtained by laser ablation of a mixed Si/SiO‚ÇÇ target. XAFS measurements at the Si K-edge of the as-deposited samples and of samples with the oxide removed showed that the core of the NW maintains the diamond local structure, with increased disorder compared to bulk Si. The same group has also more recently studied [152] Si NW using PLY detection, identifying luminescence bands originating from the quantum-confined silicon crystallites, the encapsulating silicon oxide and the silicon‚Äìoxide interface.

Finally, we mention XANES studies on silicon nanodots embedded in a SiO‚ÇÇ film, performed by recording TEY and PLY spectra by the Trento group [153]. The comparison has allowed to propose and validate a model for the origin of the observed enhanced photoluminescence. Both experimental and theoretical results show that the interface between the silicon nanocrystals and the surrounding SiO‚ÇÇ is not sharp: an intermediate region of amorphous nature and variable composition links the Si to amorphous stoichiometric SiO‚ÇÇ. This region plays an active role in the light-emission process.

## 3.3 Nitride heterostructures and thin films
GaN and related materials have been intensely studied in recent years in view of the properties which make them attractive for applications: a wide band gap (in the blue‚Äìviolet region), good thermal and chemical stability, and physical hardness. The most common crystal structure of nitrides is the hexagonal wurtzite one but cubic forms are also possible, albeit metastable. The wurtzite structure grows in the (0001) direction and therefore has a polar character which entails strong polarization fields (both intrinsic and piezoelectric). The cubic and hexagonal polytypes of GaN are very similar when viewed along the cubic (111) and hexagonal (0001) directions and because the formation of stacking faults is energetically favorable, stabilization of the hexagonal phase is sometimes difficult.

An important problem for this class of materials is the absence of substrates with comparable lattice parameters, crystal symmetry and thermal expansion coefficients; the most commonly used substrates are Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ, SiC, and Si. XAFS investigations have contributed to clarify the local atomic structure of nitride alloys, determine the local effect of strain due to heteroepitaxial growth, investigate the relation between the relative fraction of cubic and hexagonal phases of GaN films and the choice of substrate, and finally study the fine details of the local structure of GaN.

Miyano et al. [154] studied the variation of interatomic distances in wurtzite Al‚ÇìGa‚ÇÅ‚Äì‚ÇìN alloys deposited by metal organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) on sapphire, with x in the range from 0 to 0.43. The films were about 1 ¬µm thick, so no effect of the strain due to the growth was expected; XRD showed a linear variation of the a and c lattice parameters of the wurtzite structure as a function of x. Ga K-edge EXAFS allowed the authors to measure the interatomic distances in the first and second coordination shells around Ga, finding that the first-shell Ga‚ÄìN bond lengths exhibit a variation with x which is much weaker than the variation of the lattice parameters. Essentially, this study extends to the wurtzite structure the classical results [104,105] on zinc blende III‚ÄìV pseudobinary alloys.

The effect of strain due to heteroepitaxial growth was probed in two studies of GaN ultrathin films grown by molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) on SiC substrates [155] and AlN layers [156], both of which exploited the polarization dependence of the X-ray absorption cross section (see discussion in Section 1.3) to decouple the local structure parallel and perpendicular to the growth plane. For the case of SiC substrates the growth was found to be always relaxed, while the GaN on AlN films were found to be partially strained, due to the lower lattice mismatch; the degree of strain was found to depend on the substrate temperature and the ratio between the strain parallel and perpendicular to the interface was found to be in good agreement with estimates on the basis of the available elastic constants and macroscopic elasticity theory. These studies can be viewed as an extension to nitride epilayers of the previously described ones for III‚ÄìV systems (see Section 3.1); it must be mentioned that nitride epilayers are intrinsically more defective than III‚ÄìVs, so an understanding of local distortions due to the heteroepitaxial growth is extremely valuable.

The effect of the substrate on the crystal structure of GaN thin films has been studied by Paloura‚Äôs group [157,158]. By using the polarization dependence of N K-edge XANES spectra, these authors were able to illustrate how an Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ(0001) substrate yields a wurtzite GaN film, that a Si(001) substrate yields a cubic film, and that a Si(111) substrate yields a mixed phase. The physical basis of this kind of study is that the atomic absorption cross section must exhibit (at least) the symmetry of the point group of the crystal structure in which the absorbing atom is embedded [159]. For zinc blende films the point group is T_d and the cross section is isotropic. For wurtzite films, instead, the point group is C‚ÇÜv and the angular dependence of the cross section is given by

GaN films are highly defective: in addition to stacking faults that give rise to both cubic and hexagonal phases, point defects such as N vacancies and Ga antisites occur at relatively high concentrations. ### 3.4. Dilute alloy heterostructures
In recent years there has been considerable interest in dilute alloy heterostructures, obtained by adding a concentration on the order of 10‚Åª¬≥‚Äì10‚Åª¬≤ of a foreign atom to the epilayer. Two materials systems will be reviewed below: dilute nitrides and diluted magnetic semiconductors (DMSs). In these fields, XAFS has played an important role both as an atomic-level characterization tool‚Äîproviding feedback to optimize growth procedures‚Äîand for understanding physical properties on the basis of local atomic structure.

#### 3.4.1. Dilute nitrides
The incorporation of small quantities (0.1‚Äì1 %) of an isoelectronic impurity with very different electronegativity and size into III‚ÄìV semiconductor alloys causes dramatic and unexpected changes in physical properties. For example, adding nitrogen to GaAs, InGaAs or GaP leads to a giant (and counterintuitive) band-gap reduction and a lattice‚Äêparameter decrease. These effects make dilute nitrides attractive for light emitters in the telecommunications wavelength range and for high‚Äêefficiency solar cells. XAFS has been used to study atomic ordering in quaternary dilute nitrides and the structure of complexes formed upon hydrogen irradiation.

In the quaternary alloy (InGa)(AsN), cation and anion sublattices can be occupied randomly by In/Ga and As/N, respectively. This gives rise to questions about the degree of atomic ordering‚Äîi.e., the relative number of each bond type as a function of composition. In the low-N limit, short-range ordering (SRO) can manifest as samples having exclusively N‚ÄìIn or N‚ÄìGa bonds. Kim and Zunger [165] predicted a strong preference for In‚ÄìN over Ga‚ÄìN bonds, which would cause a significant blue shift of the band gap‚Äîcounter to the red shift of applicative interest.

XAFS findings in 2003:
- Lordi et al. [166] concluded that the as‚Äêdeposited (InGa)(AsN) alloy has a random atomic distribution, while annealing increases the relative number of In‚ÄìN bonds, moving the system toward a more stable SRO state.
- Uno et al. [167] reported similar qualitative trends upon annealing.
- Ciatto et al. [168,169] provided a quantitative analysis combining In K-edge EXAFS and N K-edge XANES. They compared the In‚ÄìN coordination number with N concentrations measured by nuclear reaction analysis and found that as‚Äêdeposited samples are random, whereas annealed samples exhibit a statistically significant‚Äîbut less pronounced than predicted‚Äîdegree of SRO.

Another remarkable property of dilute nitrides is that hydrogen irradiation fully reverses the nitrogen‚Äêinduced changes: XRD of hydrogenated GaAsN shows disappearance of the GaAsN diffraction peaks, recovery of the GaAs lattice parameter, and reopening of the band gap. XAFS has been decisive in elucidating the nature of the hydrogen complexes responsible for these effects, through combined N K-edge XANES measurements and full-multiple-scattering simulations based on ab initio atomic coordinates.complexes in hydrogenated GaAsN, Ciatto et al. provided convincing evidence that dihydrogen‚Äìnitrogen complexes with C‚ÇÇv symmetry are the most abundant. This finding contradicted all previous predictions of ‚Äúin-line‚Äù N‚ÄìH‚ÇÇ* complexes as the predominant species. In Fig. 16(a) we sketch the structures of some of the hydrogen complexes and in Fig. 16(b) the comparison between the simulations and the experiment; the best agreement is clearly found for the complexes with C‚ÇÇv symmetry. This chapter nicely illustrates the high-energy resolution in XANES measurements which can be obtained in the soft X-ray range, the three-dimensional sensitivity of XANES, and the power of combined experimental‚Äìsimulation studies.

### 3.4.2. Dilute magnetic semiconductors
A dilute magnetic semiconductor (DMS) consists of a nonmagnetic semiconductor doped with a few percent of a magnetic ion (a transition metal, e.g., Mn or Fe). DMS thin films are typically obtained by molecular-beam epitaxy (MBE) growth on an appropriate substrate. In a DMS, the ions‚Äîideally occupying substitutional sites in the semiconductor lattice‚Äîretain ferromagnetic alignment in the presence of spin-polarized free carriers.  

DMSs have been studied since the 1970s. Initially much work was done on II‚ÄìVI-based alloys, later on III‚ÄìV semiconductors, and more recently on nitrides; DMSs form the material basis of the emerging field of spintronics.

There are expectations that room-temperature ferromagnetism might be obtained in DMSs. Indeed, in 2000 Dietl et al. predicted that room-temperature ferromagnetism could be obtained by doping GaN or ZnO with a few percent of Mn. Three years later, ferromagnetism at 172 K was reported for p-type Œ¥-doped GaAs:Mn and, in 2005, the same group reported a Curie temperature of 250 K in a specially designed heterostructure.

A great amount of work has been reported on DMSs; much of it is very recent and the panorama of results is continuously changing. Sample deposition procedures are far from optimized, which makes it difficult to draw certain conclusions. For the nitride- and oxide-based systems, the reader is referred to the recent and extensive review by Chambers for a materials science perspective. XAFS has played an important role in studies of DMSs, both initially at the level of basic characterization and then to gain insight into the relation between local structure and physical properties. This is also due to the rich chemistry and variety of oxidation states of transition metal ions, which can be accurately characterized by the technique. Moreover, it is important to note that ferromagnetism can also be observed if ferromagnetic precipitates are present in the material, but this does not qualify as a true DMS; hence, an atomic-level probe such as XAFS is of crucial value. For reasons of space, this section is limited to Mn-doped III‚ÄìV semiconductors and GaN, which are probably the most intensely studied class of DMSs.

As is often the case, the first studies in the field were of a preliminary character. For InAs:Mn, Krol et al. reported that only a small fraction of Mn atoms was actually substitutional in the InAs lattice and most formed MnAs clusters. A few years later, using optimized sample preparation procedures and more accurate data analysis, the same group clearly highlighted the role of concentration and growth temperature: at low (< 1 %) Mn concentration and low growth temperature, substitutional Mn was clearly detected, while clusters with the local structure of MnAs precipitated at higher concentrations or substrate temperatures. The origin of this behavior is the limited solubility of Mn in III‚ÄìV semiconductors; to avoid the formation of MnAs inclusions, the growth temperature for Mn-doped III‚ÄìVs has to be between 200 ¬∞C and 300 ¬∞C, i.e., very low compared to the 500‚Äì650 ¬∞C interval used for standard GaAs-based materials.

For the prototypical GaAs:Mn system, more work has been reported. In this system, there is a close interplay between the local structure of Mn and the electronic properties. By using DFT simulations, Mahadevan and Zunger predicted that the formation energy of an interstitial Mn defect decreases as the Fermi level shifts toward the valence band, which can occur as a consequence of Be codoping. Shioda et al. initially reported complete Mn incorporation in substitutional sites for concentrations below 7 % and growth temperatures of 250‚Äì300 ¬∞C; they measured a Mn‚ÄìAs bond length of 2.50 √Ö, which is 0.05 √Ö higher than the value for Ga‚ÄìAs and thus implies considerable local distortion. Later, Bacewicz et al. studied samples grown between 220 ¬∞C and 250 ¬∞C with Mn concentrations below 4 %; by a combined analysis of the XANES and EXAFS regions they detected a significant fraction of interstitial Mn atoms, though no correlation with other sample properties was reported.

More complex GaAs:Mn-based heterostructures have also been studied. Two methods were recently proposed as alternatives to the growth of bulk GaAs:Mn alloys: 
- **Digital GaAs/MnAs heterostructures**, obtained by alternately depositing layers of GaAs and MnAs.  
- **Œ¥-doping of GaAs with Mn**, consisting of creating a Œ¥-like doping profile in the growth direction by inserting in GaAs a submonolayer-thick Mn layer.  

In both cases, the basic idea is to achieve locally higher Mn concentrations where high hole densities are desired.Soo et al. [182,183] have studied Mn/GaAs digital alloys. They found that in samples deposited at the low temperature of 275 ¬∞C, Mn substitutes Ga and locally forms a GaMnAs alloy; upon annealing, a dramatic decrease of the first‚Äêshell coordination number was found and the authors suggested that this was due to initial stages of precipitation of a MnAs phase. Later, the same group published [184] a convincing study, which combined the local structural sensitivity of Mn K-edge XAFS with an atom-specific magnetic probe such as X-ray magnetic circular dichroism (XMCD) performed at the Mn L-edge. They found a high ferromagnetic alignment of the Mn atoms and a local structure exclusively composed of substitutional Mn in the Ga sites, as in a random (GaMn)As alloy. More recently, d‚ÄôAcapito et al. [185] have studied a series of Mn d-doped GaAs samples, deposited at growth temperatures in the range 300‚Äì450 ¬∞C, with and without Be codoping. In low-temperature samples, Mn was confirmed to be substitutional to Ga. Interestingly, a direct correlation between Be codoping (which lowers the Fermi level toward the valence band) and the presence of Mn interstitials was demonstrated; this study illustrates the ability of XAFS to detect relatively small fractions of defective sites and to measure their local structure quantitatively.

XAFS has also made a significant contribution for the characterization of Mn-doped GaN films; again, it should be remarked that the nitrides are a more defective materials system than III‚ÄìVs and that a significant evolution in the near future is expected as the close interplay between growth and characterization will lead to improved materials. XAFS has provided important results on the site of Mn in GaN and its oxidation state.

Soo et al. [186] studied 150-nm-thick films deposited by MBE on Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ(0001) at substrate temperatures ranging between 400 ¬∞C and 650 ¬∞C, with a maximum Mn concentration of 2 %; magnetic measurements indicated that the samples were paramagnetic. EXAFS measurements indicated that Mn occupied a substitutional Ga site with a Ga‚ÄìN bond length which was found to be ‚àº0.07 √Ö greater than the Ga‚ÄìN one. XANES measurements on the samples were compared to several oxide and fluoride reference compounds in which Mn has known oxidation states and it was concluded that Mn has the +2 oxidation state. While this is certainly a reasonable conclusion, it should be remarked that, ideally, such comparisons should be performed with reference compounds of known structure and composition as close as possible to the samples; it is an unfortunate situation that this is not strictly possible with Mn-doped GaN.

In 2004, Thaler et al. [187] reported EXAFS data on Mn-doped GaN films grown by MBE on either Al‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ(0001) or MOCVD GaN substrates with and without a 2-¬µm-thick GaN buffer layer. Samples grown at 700 ¬∞C with the GaN buffer layers exhibited strong magnetization even above room temperature (but no remanence). The EXAFS measurements indicated that Mn was always substitutional, independently of the presence of the buffer layer; this result indicated the important role of the defect density of the GaN matrix in determining the magnetic behavior.

One of the concerns in the growth of Mn-doped GaN is the presence of multiple oxidation states of Mn and their spatial distribution. In this context, micro-XAFS has provided results that nicely illustrate the potential of the technique. By using an intense spot size of the order of 1 ¬µm¬≤, Mart√≠nez-Criado et al. [188] performed a joint microfluorescence mapping and micro-XAFS experiment. Mapping of the Mn and Ga fluorescence intensity demonstrated their inhomogeneous distribution. Moreover, by performing maps of fluorescence intensity at specific excitation energies which correspond to XANES features of Mn in the Mn‚Å∞, Mn¬≤‚Å∫, and Mn¬≥‚Å∫ oxidation states (chosen using as references metallic Mn, MnO, and Mn‚ÇÇO‚ÇÉ) the authors were able to show the presence in the samples investigated of all three oxidation states, thus including metallic Mn, and their strongly inhomogeneous spatial distribution.

The presence of Mn¬≥‚Å∫ in a substitutional site in MBE-grown samples on a GaN buffer layer up to a concentration of 5.7 % was nicely demonstrated by Titov et al. [189] using high-resolution XANES measurements and their interpretation using band-structure calculations. This study illustrates the usefulness of simulations in cases in which an ideal reference compound does not exist. We conclude this section by quoting a combined X-ray absorption and XMCD study at the Mn L-edge by Keavney et al. [190]; by comparison with first-principles band-structure calculations, these authors propose that Mn preferentially populates Ga sites neighboring N split interstitials.

4. Summary and perspectives  
XAFS has established itself as a precious tool to study the local structure in the field of materials science. The underlying theory, which has been outlined in Section 1, is well understood and a number of reliable analysis programs are now available. Owing to the many possible detection modes, most of which have been described in Section 2, it can be applied to a great variety of systems and sensitivity to a particular phase, environment, or part of the sample can be enhanced on the basis of the experimental requirements. As a consequence, applications of XAFS to a wide variety of heterostructures and nanostructures, which have been reviewed in Section 3, have been widespread and have greatly contributed to the present understanding of the relation between atomic structure and physical properties.## Future Directions

There is no doubt that in the future XAFS will continue to be a valuable tool in this field of research. It is possible to outline specific areas in which progress is expected.

The spatial resolution of synchrotron radiation techniques has greatly improved recently [100‚Äì102], and certainly further advances will occur in the near future. Micro-XAFS is expected to be very valuable in two areas: characterization of inhomogeneous samples (with possible feedback to sample growth) and study of the local atomic environment of individual nanostructures. Concerning the last point, the challenge is to record spectra of sufficient quality and energy range to allow a quantitative interpretation; a strong effort is required in this area. If this issue is resolved, it might be possible to perform ‚Äúbond length maps‚Äù of nanostructures.

Time-resolved XAFS has recently emerged as a very promising field, thanks to the full use of the time structure of storage rings and the pump-and-probe scheme (for a review, see Ref. [191]). Differential XANES spectra of a metastable charge-transfer state of an organometallic molecule with 100 ps X-ray pulses have been reported by Saes et al. [192]. A lot of work is currently underway in synchrotron radiation laboratories, and the future challenge is to use the femtosecond pulses of LINAC-based sources. Present time resolutions range many orders of magnitude, from seconds to picoseconds [191]. In semiconductor science, there have been only limited applications of time-resolved XAFS‚Äîfor example, studies of photoexcitation of GaAs [193] and of melting of Si [194]. More work in this area is expected, for instance to study the kinetics of annealing-induced bonding changes, crystallization and amorphization, and aggregation and growth of nanostructures in real time.

Work is also in progress on interesting developments in the quantitative interpretation of the XANES region. XANES spectra contain a wealth of information, especially in view of the three-dimensional sensitivity; at present, even most advanced work in the solid state relies on a comparison between simulations and experiments, with semi-quantitative agreement. It would be a significant advancement to develop fitting methods‚Äîsimilar to those devised in the field of biomolecules [31]‚Äîfor the quantitative determination of the three-dimensional structure of very small nanostructures, defects, and complexes in semiconductors. Finally, great progress is being made in ab initio structural simulations, with methods based on DFT theory. The accuracy of these methods and the reliability of XAFS analysis are such that joint experimental‚Äìtheoretical investigations are now possible [30,171]. Simulations of the local atomic structure can be useful as a starting point for XAFS analysis, as a guide in the analysis itself, and as a very useful comparison with expected results. Further progress will certainly take place in both theory and experiment, in both the EXAFS and the XANES regions.
